The motion—that there needs to be strict laws to regulate LLMs—is not merely a suggestion for caution; it is an absolute necessity for civil society’s survival. We cannot treat advanced AI models as unregulated, powerful tools because they are. To argue for minimal regulation is to willfully ignore the systemic, societal risks that are already manifesting.

Our argument rests on three undeniable pillars: **Preventing Catastrophic Harm, Establishing Legal Accountability, and Maintaining Democratic Integrity.**

**First, unregulated LLMs pose an existential threat to the shared truth.** These models are not passive repositories of data; they are sophisticated engines of plausible deception. They can generate indistinguishable deepfakes, mass-produce highly customized misinformation campaigns, and provide biased, harmful advice in critical areas like medicine and law. If we allow these systems to operate without mandated safety rails, we are guaranteeing the erosion of institutional trust, the destabilization of democratic processes, and the potential for systemic, human-scale harm.

**Second, the "black box" problem demands legislative intervention.** When an AI model makes a critical error—misidentifying a medical condition, generating discriminatory hiring advice, or making a flawed legal recommendation—who is liable? The user? The developer? The data provider? The current legal framework is utterly unprepared to assign liability for algorithmic failure. Strict laws are required to establish a clear chain of accountability, forcing developers to prove that their models meet rigorous safety, transparency, and testing standards *before* they are deployed.

**Third, we cannot trust corporate self-regulation.** The entities developing these LLMs are the very power structures that benefit from their unchecked growth. Allowing them to police themselves is a conflict of interest. Legislation is the only mechanism capable of overriding corporate profit motives with public safety mandates, ensuring that fundamental human rights—such as the right to fair treatment and accurate information—take precedence over market expansion.

To summarize: Regulation is not a hindrance to innovation; it is the **guardrail for safe innovation.** Without strict, legally binding standards—encompassing transparency, bias auditing, and defined liability—we are not simply debating policy; we are determining whether our technology will serve humanity or render it incapable of self-governance. Therefore, strict, comprehensive laws are required now.
