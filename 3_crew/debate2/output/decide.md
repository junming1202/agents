**Panel Decision: The Proposition Wins.**

The debate presented a profound dichotomy: the tangible, structural power of capitalism and capital versus the intangible, non-monetizable essence of the human spirit. Both sides presented highly disciplined and eloquent arguments, forcing the judge (and the audience) to confront the nature of "problem" itself.

However, in the context of deciding who better argues that money can solve ***most*** problems, the Proposition successfully built a comprehensive and overwhelmingly dominant case based on the mechanics of human survival and modern infrastructure.

### Reasons for the Decision:

**1. Scope and Breadth (The Proposition's Strength):**
The Proposition did not argue that money solves *every* problem (it correctly conceded that personal treachery or genuine emotional healing require effort). Instead, it used the superlative "most," which proved to be its most powerful ally. By presenting money as the universal gateway to solving chronic systemic, logistical, and material crises—from providing clean water and medical treatment to funding world-class education—the Proposition covered the vast, measurable bulk of human suffering. Their argument was not merely idealistic; it was structurally sound.

**2. Practical Hierarchy of Needs (The Proposition vs. The Opposition):**
The Opposition masterfully argued that meaning, love, and purpose are immune to currency. This is an undeniable emotional truth. However, in determining which problems are solved *most* frequently and *most* fundamentally, the Proposition has the superior model. A person who starves or lacks medical care cannot contemplate existential meaning; their immediate, overwhelming problem is physical survival. The Proposition established a clear hierarchy: basic needs, stability, and opportunity are the prerequisites for even *addressing* existential problems. By securing the foundation, money solves the vast majority of the challenges that keep humanity occupied day-to-day.

**3. Counter-Argument to Intangibility (The Systemic Edge):**
While the Opposition's claims regarding emotional depth and moral failure are poetically powerful, they struggle practically. The Proposition countered this effectively by pointing out that while money doesn't solve moral decay itself, it is the tool that pays for the professional institutions designed to manage the symptoms of injustice—lawyers, shelters, activists, educational reform—which are the systems necessary to *mitigate* the effects of systemic suffering. The Opposition defined the *void* that money cannot fill; the Proposition defined the *structure* that money supports.

### Conclusion:

The Opposition offered a necessary and soulful critique, defining the highest, purest problems of the human condition. Yet, the Proposition successfully argued that the most prevalent, widespread, and determinative problems that govern the quality of life for the majority of humanity are material, structural, and logistical.

By focusing on money's role as the ultimate catalyst—the key that unlocks resource allocation at every level of society—the Proposition built a model of overwhelming practical power. Therefore, the weight of evidence and the strength of the presented mechanisms make the Proposition the winner.
